You Brought a Bat to a Gun Fight!

10/19/2014

(Hint--the title was just an excuse to link to this awesome page from the Walking Dead comic. SPOILER WARNING!)

Oh, snap! It's time to talk about conflict. No, not this kind of conflict.

I'm talking about the more common kind of conflict--when you and another person just aren't clicking--maybe they were offended by something you said--maybe you don't like their work style--maybe they totally stole your awesome fashion idea. It happens.

The question, of course, is what do we do when we face those kinds of things? It's never fun--but we've got deal with it--or it's gonna get worse. Rarely, if ever, do conflicts resolve themselves.

I've been in the middle of this kind of thing before. A couple of years ago, I was a part of a group of people working on starting a new company--I won't go into specifics because they're not necessary. There were three of us who were really in charge of the big picture--see, we were merging two different groups together and things were pretty sensitive.

At the end of the day, the problem was between myself and another member of the three. We got along fine, but he and I had some pretty different values when it came to how we thought the business should be run. This was a creative business--and the problem of what kinds of products we would produce together was of the utmost importance.

Things quickly devolved into political crap. It was lame. Terrible, really. I had some really ambitious ideas--ideas that I thought would make us distinct in our market and create interest.

The other guy had a more mainstream view on what the company should produce that I thought was boring (still do). His ideas weren't bad--but they weren't at all in line with what I wanted to do. They were traditional--which is typically the exact opposite of what I want.

At the end of the day, no one got what they wanted--the company split back up into the original two groups, and within a year, both groups fell apart. Maybe we wouldn't have been successful even if we had stayed together. Can't really tell with that kind of thing.

I don't regret sticking up for myself and my ideas. I trust my gut and my artistic sensibilities. But there is something I do regret: I initially agreed to compromise on some things that I internally knew I couldn't compromise on. Wouldn't. I thought that once things were more established, more real, I could convince this guy to see things from my perspective. Because I was so smart and charming, you know?

That sounds pretty stupid now. If I could give any advice to past-Jordy, I'd tell him to be sincere when you're discussing what you want. Communicate. Be real. Be honest. You'll either get what you want, find a good compromise, or realize early on that things aren't going to work out. And any of those are less stressful and emotional than trying to force a merger between two groups with completely opposing values that aren't negotiable.

But past-Jordy wasn't the brightest light on the Christmas tree. I'm not sure he would have listened. He probably needed the experience.

Sometimes you've gotta blow off some fingers to know to be careful with fireworks.
